We review the Volvo V40 D2 R-Design from price to economy and all its features

 Posted on May 16, 2024  |  3 minutes  |  465 words  |  Martina Birk

VOLVO is the safest car firm in the world. No question.

After testing the Volvo V40 D2 R-Design, I realise I’ve been a fool for stereotyping Volvo drivers all these years.

The V40's appearance immediately stokes feelings, and encourages drivers to get behind the wheel.

Yesteryear stereotypes about Volvo drivers disappear as fast as a well-sucked Werther's Original.

I want to take this car to a track. I want to drive it hard. These feelings accelerate as I hit the motorway.

Why? Because of how safe I feel. I'm in a Volvo, I think I’m invincible.

This is partly because of the years of marketing that Volvo have thrown at me, but also because of the bewildering new tech in the V40.

 This feels reassuringly safe. It breeds confidence in you and your car

For example, the windscreen glows red when I get too close to the car in front. And the wing mirrors light red when someone is creeping towards a blind spot.

Or the little correction I get from the steering wheel, as if the V40 is just politely asking: "Did you mean to drift out of the middle lane and into the fast lane?" Well, yes I did, thank you very much.

This does not feel like health and safety gone mad. This feels reassuringly safe. It breeds confidence in you and your car. I need to take this car to a test track.

This Volvo appreciation should not make me sound like an old man. Because I'm not, I'm 43.
But with this kind of safety kit and engine power at my fingertips, the Volvo V40 has left me aching to push this car to the limits - and the chequered flag. Maybe I should calm down and have that Werthers!

Key Facts

  • Price: £26,520
  • Engine: 1.6-litre 115bhp
  • Economy: 74.3mpg
  • Road tax: £0 with CO2 emissions of 99g/km
  • Service intervals: 1yr/12.500 miles
  • Warranty: 3yrs/60,000 miles 0-62mph: 11.9secs
  • Top speed: 115mph

 

Volvo’s vision of crash-free future

Boffins at Volvo have opened a new testing ground, to help them achieve their goal of zero deaths or serious injuries in new Volvos by 2020.

AstaZero in Sweden is the world’s first full-scale proving ground for future traffic safety solutions.

The new £43m centre can simulate a wide range of conditions - busy city roads, highways, multi-lane motorways and crossroads.

‪The work at AstaZero will also include the development and testing of autonomous driving tech. And advanced systems are also being developed to further help prevent inattentiveness and driver fatigue.

Volvo’s Anders Axelson is confident the centre will help motorists, pedestrians and cyclists alike: “Thanks to AstaZero, we have great prospects for keeping our leading position. We’re the only car manufacturing company in the world to have set a goal of zero traffic fatalities for a specific date.”
